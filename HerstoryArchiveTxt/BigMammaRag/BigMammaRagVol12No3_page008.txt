When she was in New Zealand  
in August Madhu Kishwar,  
from India, talked to Papa-  
rangi Reid about Indian  

land rights struggles and  
the position of the tribals.  
(Reprinted from Broadsheet,

Can you tell us about the  
tribals? Who are they and  
what are their struggles?

The census lists about 7%  
of the 700 million popula-  
tion of India as tribals.  
They are still ethnically  
distinct and in many parts  
of the country they have  
preserved their separate  
ethnic identity. In some  
parts of the country there  
are distinct tribal pockets,  
where the tribals would con-  
stitute the subsistence  
peasantry. Regions such as  
the Charkan region covering  
the states of Bihar, Madhya  
Pradesh and Orissa in cen-  
tral India are now demanding  
a separate state for them-  
selves. That is one of the  
areas which is very politi-  
cally explosive.

What is the basis of the  
movements? Is it the land?

It is a lot to do with the  
land. It is also to do with  
forest rights, it is to do  
with oppression, with deman-  
ding a better deal for tri-  
bals. Though sometimes the  
way it is expressed is a  
little chaotic. The north  
hill regions also have sev-  
eral pockets which have sub-  
stantial tribal populations.  
There are similarly tribal  
pockets in the south of In-  
dia, in Andhra Pradesh and  
Tamil Nadu, but in many of these  
areas, for instance in Maha-  
rashtra, which is on the  
west coast, they have been  
overruled. They lost their  
land in the 19th century at  
the time of various settle-  
ment operations carried out  
by the British, so today  
they constitute the landless  
poor. Similarly, in large  
parts of Andhra Pradesh,  
the tribals would be the  
landless poor. So it varies,  
either they would consti-  
tute the subsistence pea-  
santry, the poor peasantry,  
even when they are in own-  
ership of land, or they are  
the landless poor, living

What are the problems of  
the landless poor and wom-  
en's role in that struggle?

In most parts of the  
country, the landless poor  
are usually organised  
around the struggle for bet-  
ter wages. They are often  
paid less than subsistence  
wages, less even than the  
minimum wage and women paid  
less than men. So you can  
imagine what that means.  
There are also semi-bonded  
conditions. The poor would  
be made to perform semi-  
compulsory forms of labour  
in the fields of the landed  
rich. Because they are so  
poor, very often they have  
to be indebted and if you  
are indebted and the rates  
of interest are exorbitant,  
in the countryside it is  
despairing even to think  
of. In some parts, it has  
been found that a man may  
have borrowed two bags of  
rice or a few rupees two  
generations ago, in return  
for which he had to become  
a bonded labourer. In the  
next generation and the  
next the interest kept pil-  
ing up. They could never  
hope to repay. The women  
would be particularly prone  
to sexual exploitation. They  
have to be made available  
in some parts of the coun-  
try, for instance, the tra-  
dition of the right of the  
first night. Or even, if  
the man's wife is pregnant,  
he expects to be supplied  
with somebody else. Or they  
would be kept as mistresses.  
Landlessness, indebtedness  
and sexual exploitation are  
so intertwined with each  
other, that you could hardly  
say where force ends and  
consent begins.
