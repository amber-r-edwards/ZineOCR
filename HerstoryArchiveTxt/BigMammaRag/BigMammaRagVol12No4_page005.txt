Paula Three Stars

(This article was edited
from articles printed in
No More Cages and North-
west Passage.)

Paula Three Stars, a 30-
year-old Oglala Sioux woman,
was charged with first-
degree murder for the shoot-
ing of her common-law hus-
band of six and a half
years, Daniel B. (Sonny)
Evening, Jr., during a
battering episode on March
12, 1984.

Paula suffered physical
abuse from Sonny throughout
their relationship. Sonny
was a heavy user of both
alcohol and other drugs.

When Sonny was in an abusive
mood, Paula would try to
hide herself in the house
and garage but gradually
Sonny discovered all her
hiding spots, according to
an acquaintance of Paula's.

Paula called the police
for help during one of the
battering episodes but was
told there was "nothing they
could do." Realizing the
police would not respond to
a domestic dispute, Paula
called again and reported
a robbery. The police res-
ponded immediately, but in-
stead of arresting Sonny,
they arrested Paula for
making a false report. In-
stead of being taken to a
shelter, she was taken to
jail.

A week before the shoot-
ing, Paula sought refuge
with a neighbor after her
husband had taken a shot
at her. Paula, fearing for
her life, shot Sonny during
a battering episode on the
night of March 12.

Paula Three Stars, a
mother of two, former ex-
ecutive secretary of a
Northwest Indian Health
Board and former nurse's
aide, has the support of
her family, community, NOW,
the Equal Defense Alliance
(EDA), the Northwest Indian
Women's Circle, Leonard
Peltier Defense Committee
and Women of All Red Na-
tions (San Francisco). The
EDA, founded in 1980, is
based on the premise of the
basic right for a woman to
act in self-defense.

A week before
trial was to begin, she
accepted a plea bargaining
offer from the prosecutor.
She pleaded guilty to first-
degree manslaughter, a
lesser crime. The prosecu-
tor is recommending proba-
tion.

In a statement following
her decision, Paula said
that she is innocent and
had acted in self-defense.
She accepted the plea bar-
gain since she didn't be-
lieve she would receive a
fair trial due to her sex
and race. This ordeal has
made Paula "more aware that
I wasn't the only one (bat-
tered woman). It makes me
sad to know that I wasnâ€™t
the only one - it's a hell
of a comfort - but it has
really snapped my eyes open.
I'm determined that if I
can ever help someone in
that situation, I sure
will."

Paula's sentencing hear-

ing will take place April

13 in the Snohomish County
Superior Court, Washington.
