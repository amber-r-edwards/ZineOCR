8. Book ordering procedure.
A) There should be a com-
mittee to order books. This
will allow different views
and ideas to be represented.
B) Books should cover a wide
range of topics relating to
women's issues. These should
include positive images of
women and children in the
areas of sexuality, culture,
work, fiction, psychology,
biographies, sociology, pol-
itical theory, lesbian mot-
hers, family, creativity and
the arts, self-help, contro-
versial issues, law, poetry,
philosophy, women of color
and Jewish women. We should
try to concentrate on the
issues that isolate women
from each other thereby pro-
viding support for each other
in our individual yet common
struggles.
C) The shelf list should
represent - equally - the
struggles of all women in
various aspects of their
lives.
D) The books should be inte-
grated throughout the shelves.
Women's lives and struggles
are not segmented as currently
represented on the shelves.
Books relating to Third World
people need not only be on a
Third World shelf.
E) The review policy should
be changed to involve the
community. Books would be
put on the shelves without
necessarily being previously
reviewed by collective mem-
bers. We would solicit women
from the community to help
us review the books. This
would enable the community
to be an active part of the
bookstore and would not all-
ow the bookstore to become
stagnant and narrow-minded in
its book ordering.
F) Censorship in the areas of
racism, sexism and classism
would continue. A section on
controversial topics would be
maintained and we would soli-
cit response from the com-
munity on whether to keep them
in the store. We should not
discourage women from obtain-
ing information that is per-
tinent to their lives.
G) Books should be ordered be-
cause of the information they
contain, this may necessitate
that we order books written
by men. Some subjects are so
important that any informa-
tion is welcome and having
them missing from the store
hurts us all.

All women present discussed
the issues that were presented
in the paper and demands. They
went through the points and
at Point 6 they decided that
two groups of women should
meet immediately afterwards;
one group to deal with an al-
ternative to a paid staffer
and the other group to prepare
a budget to find money for
paying a staffwoman. Another
meeting was scheduled for
June 18.

On June 18 the meeting was
chaired by a woman of color
who requested that everyone in
the room talk about what they
were feeling. Most everyone
there said that they thought,
on the whole, it was necessary
to acknowledge that changes
had to be made so that the
center would be a real place
for women to come and not feel
left out or put down and that
the changes, as listed in the
demands, for the most part,
would be supported. Point 6,
the issue of a paid staffer,
was by far the one that most
women were upset about and the
group that wrote the paper on
alternatives read that paper.

The paper on alternatives
was heard and denounced by the
women present. The paper it-
self, the women felt, was ra-
cist. Criticism was made that
the wording, which referred to
women of color, but did not
mention Jewish women was a ra-
cist, anti-Semitic act unto
itself. Women were angry that
the group who wrote the alter-
natives had a hand-written
paper that was not distributed
around the room. The women
said that had happened because
they were pressed for time.
They apologized for not in-
cluding "Jewish women" when
they addressed women of color
but it was pointed out that
they never addressed Jewish
women throughout the paper and
that women of color and Jewish
women who wrote the paper had
made a point of having typed
copies of both their paper and
their demands available for
everyone to have and they too
had been pressed for time.

The paper, which made frequ-
ent use of the word empower-
ment sounded as if white women
would give power, let women of
color learn how to function in
positions of power in the cen-
ter. The alternatives present-
ed in the paper was the struc-
ture that already existed at
the center but explicitly in-
cluded women of color. One
section dealing with outreach
would have had women of color
as the group that would do the
outreach and women of color
felt that, again, they would
have to do the work of white
women to combat their racism.
Also mention was made of the
time that the center funded
some women of color to attend
a conference in San Francisco.
This was denounced by women
of color and Jewish women who
felt that the women were very
minimally funded and that
hassles occurred when the women
returned from the conference
and the racism of that had
never been dealt with.

Women of color and Jewish
women felt that having a paid
staffer was not the best al-
ternative to have in order to
keep the center open, but it
was the only practical method
that they could think of. Oth-
er women did not come up with
anything better and women who
fought against the idea be-
cause of political experience
didn't have any suggestions.
The politics of such an act
was discussed but in the end
it was decided that money would
have to be raised to pay a
staffer and give the plan a
chance. Pledges were made by
many women, not only to give
a monthly amount but also
for fundraising.

Women who would have staf-
fed the center said they
couldn't do so, because as
things stood, they didn't
know the politics of the
center, and many women felt
the center should be closed
temporarily until the group
had formed and could put the
politics out for the commun-
ity to see. Other women, re-
alizing the need for money
felt the center should stay
open, also feeling that wom-
en in the community had come
to depend on the services and
would be let down if those
services weren't available.
Women of color and Jewish wo-
men felt that those services
weren't made available to
everyone. It was decided that
the center would be closed
for one week, hopefully.
Meetings were scheduled for
Saturday and Monday through
Friday to work out the nec-
essary politics.

As of press time, the group
of women meeting plan to open
the center on July 1. Local
women are urged to call the
center to make sure, call
320-5972.

In this issue, we devoted a lot of space to "local" pro-
