Introduction  

Violence against women in America takes many forms--verbal, physical, sex-  
ual, and underlying all, psychological. "Of the multiple assaults by men on  
women, none are more feared, none more vicious or traumatic, than the crimes  
of rape and sexual brutality."  

Rape is the most frequently committed crime against American women today.  

A reported rape occurs every four minutes in this country; 619 rapes were  
recorded in Denver in 1978. Many reported rapes are dismissed and never tal-  
lied, due to â€œlack of sufficient evidence". In addition, only a fraction of  
all rapes are reported; estimates run as high as ten unreported rapes for  
every one reported.  

No woman is immune from rape. The fear of rape affects all women; it inhi-  
bits their actions and limits their freedom. It affects the routes they take  
and the hours they keep.  

However, there has been a conspiracy of silence surrounding the whole issue  
of rape. The topic has either been considered taboo, sensationalized or treat-  
ed as a joke. Recognition of rape as a widespread and serious problem was  
initiated by the women's movement in the late 1960s.  

Feminists continue to challenge the basic assumptions which dominate offi-  
cial attitudes toward rape and rape victims. We demand that rape be viewed in  
a social, economic and political context.  

Those in control of our society have a vested interest in maintaining the  
status quo; "in order to preserve and enhance male supremacy, rape must be  
both possible and probable--it must remind women who has the power over them  
and keep them solidly in their places". All men in a patriarchal society ben-  
efit from rape as a part of the process of intimidation that keeps all women  
in a state of fear.  

This article will examine the relationships between rape and the patriarchy.  
The ways in which the law enforcement, medical and judicial systems reinforce  
violence against women are outlined. The methods women have devised for com-  
bating assault are discussed. The role women are playing in eradicating rape  
from society is explored.  

The Crime  

"In rape, the emotions of aggression, hatred, contempt and the desire to  
break or violate personality, take a form consummately appropriate to sexual  
politics." ~Kate Millett in Sexual Politics  

"It's the final humiliation, the final showing you that you're worthless and  
that you're there to be used by whoever wants you. In general, I think rape  
is a political act on the part of the man." -rape victim  

What is rape? The word "rape" has a variety of connotations depending upon  
the user's perspective. The symbolic use of the word - the "rape of the  
earth and its natural resources - has been overdramatized and has contributed  
to the conspiracy of silence surrounding the true definition. The number of  
books in the Denver Public Library dealing with this symbolic defini-  
tion of rape outnumber those dealing with it in its human context.  

In this article, rape will be defined as any sexual intimacy forced on a fe-  
male by a male. Rape is the deprivation of a woman's sexual self-determina-  
tion; it is the male expression of all the hatred, contempt and oppression  
of females in the society concentrated in one violent act. This includes not  
only vaginal penetration with any object (penis, finger, bottle, etc.) but  
also fellatio, cunnilingus, oral copulation, anal penetration, self-masturba-  
tion by the offender and genital manipulation of the victim by the offender.
