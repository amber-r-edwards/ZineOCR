hail mary, full of grace  

yet too slow to escape  

the staccato notes of machine gun fire  

the lord is with thee  

the twelve years of your squatter childhood  
fallen into a red pool of your own blood  

blessed art thou among women.  

though you will never know  

beyond your mother's tear-filled kiss  
the miracle of your body,  

changing with the seasons  

blessed is the fruit of thy womb  

the life you have sacrificed for this:  
the rain gutter roof for your family  
the smelly alleys lined with open sewers  
your playground, your home.  
holy mary, pray for us sinners  

the soldiers who fight brothers, sisters, parents, and cousins  
who torture and kill the young and old.  
pray for us now, mary grace  

and at this hour  

as you draw your last shuddering breath  

forgive us for trespassing  

the innocence of your childhood.

Puanani Burgess, Alice Chai, Helen Nagtalon-Miller and  
June Shimokawa also defined themselves in the opening  
panel of the Fourth Biennial Conference of the National  
Network of Asian and Pacific Women held in Honolulu in  
August. They addressed being Hawaiian-Japanese, Korean,  
Filipino and Japanese in or from Hawaii, respectively,  
on the topic, "The Asian and Pacific Woman: Who am I,  
What do I do and Why? An Effort to Understand Asian  
and Pacific Women and How They Can Work Together."  

"In many ways, I still define, identify myself in ways that  
I think has meaning for others," said June Shimokawa,  
executive director of the YWCA of Oahu for the past year,  
"by my name and that I am a Japanese-American, by my place  

of birth (the Big Island of Hawaii), my job."  
"I'd like to reach the point," she continued, "where I can  
identify myself by my beliefs and values. So that I say:  

I am a person, a woman, who values life with meaning, not  
just existence, love and justice prevailing.  

I am a person, a woman, who values fostering a sense of  
community among people who share a common space.  

I am a person, a woman, who values her Christian faith,  
which serves as her source of life, energizing her to  
act on issues of justice, with compassion.  

I am a person, a woman, who values belief in the democratic  
process."  

But until society lets us identify ourselves in those ways,  
we will need to continue to say we are "hyphenated-Americans"  
and to think of ourselves collectively--as "Asian Americans"  
and "Asian and Pacific Islanders"--in political, rather than  
cultural, terms.  

Virginia R. Cerenio and Genny Lim provide additional  
perspectives on this issue with their poems.  

In her biography, in Without Names, Virginia is described as

University (1985), Breaking Silence, Anthology of Contemporary

"Genny Lim: My Pop owned the first LaSalle in Chinatown,  
He wore a suit every day. Walked lean and erect like the  
Coit Tower. He taught me how to dance the rhumba, the  
cha-cha-cha from Prado to Puente and sang Chinese opera  
at the top of his lungs when he felt good. GUM SAN was a  
mask of cloth with Mom spreading her fingers over its  

seams night and day, stitching her way to the American  
dream. She gave birth to seven kids. I am SIU KUM (Little  
Lute), the seventh born. Second generation, Toisan, THLEE-  
YIP (fourth dialect), JOOK-SING, home-grown North Beach-San
