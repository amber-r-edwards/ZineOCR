REVIEWS

JANE SINNOTT

[movie CORNER

STAYING ALIVE: Sylvester Stallone fails in his latest effort to provide the movie-going public with another loveable loser. Tony Manero (John Travolta) is now six years older, residing in a flea bag hotel in Manhattan, waiting for his big chance to be pulled from the chorus line and given the lead in a Broadway show. His talent as a dancer is questioned by everyone. With clever editing of the boring and repetitive dance sequences, Stallone convinces the audience as well that there isn't much talent here, which makes the already thin plot even more ridiculous. Stallone cast the three female leads in the traditional roles of mother, virgin and whore, and Tony uses them all. Cynthia Rhodes, as his girlfriend, Jackie, manages to survive the horrible dialogue, and gives the warmest performance of all, but even she won't tell Tony to "shove it". The ending is an embarrassment, leaving the audience with the uncomfortable feeling that someone just played a dirty trick on us, and we were dumb enough to pay $4.00 for it.

FLASHDANCE: This low-budget film starring Jennifer Beals and Michael Nouri provides the vehicle for the "Flashdance" sequences which are the highlights of the movie, beautifully choreographed and filmed. These segments make this movie attractive to a large part of the movie-going public, under 25, who will turn on to the great score and the dancing. The otherwise simplistic plot revolves around steelworker "Alex" who is trying to find the courage to realize her dream and become a legitimate dancer. She is supported in her effort by her boss, Tony, who has realized his dream of owning the steel mill, and her friend, Jeannie, who loses her courage and will never realize her dreams. It is unfortunate that the dancing for Beals, which was done by an unknown French dancer, was not credited. The musical score, including songs by Irene Cara, Kim Carnes, and Donna Summer, is terrific and is worth the price of admission.
