The prose of Maxine Hong King-  
ston flows as in a dream, and like  
dreams, it is sometimes confusing,  
jumping from one scene or one time  
sequence to another, from the past to  
the present, from the old country to  
the newly adopted one. It seems the  
author's intent is to imitate her mo-  
ther's way of revealing a truth or  
something real through an unreal  
story or an exaggerated account of an  
actual event. And it is not imita-  
tion in the bad sense; Kingston's  
mother must have been a good story  
teller, and her daughter, assimilating  
that talent, was wise not to ig-  
nore the ability.  

The Woman Warrior is a confes-  
sional which sheds light on the tra-  
uma of a young female child growing  
up in two worlds - that of her par-  
ents, the mysterious, far away China  
with its ageless traditions and inex-  
plicable customs - and that of her  
own country, America.  

Throughout the book Kingston  
switches from the one to the other
