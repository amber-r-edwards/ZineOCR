It is important to know what sensations to expect as you are performing this. When you insert the cannula expect to feel  
some resistance. The cervical canal is very tiny, especially in the uterus that has not carried any pregnancies to term  
(nulliparas), and continued pressure will be required to introduce the cannula. Keep pushing gently until the cannula reaches  
the back wall of the uterus. (The cannula will enter the non-pregnant uterus approximately 2 to 3 inches depending upon  
uterine size and the number of pregnancies the uterus has experienced.) There will probably be some cramping when the cannula  
is inserted and you will be aware of the movement of the cannula. The sensation is strange and uncomfortable but without  
severe pain. Most women undergoing menstrual extraction experience cramping (it is considered unavoidable in the nulliparas  
uterus). The flexible cannula is incapable of damaging a normal, healthy uterus (this excludes those uteri badly scarred  
or damaged by venereal disease or traumatic childbirth) but it is still a good idea to allow your sensations to guide your movements.  
If any particular movement causes strong cramping or discomfort try replacing it with some other motion. Women who have  
extracted their menses a number of times report that the cramping following the first extraction is fairly strong, but  
that with more experience, confidence and expertise (not only in the technique but in relaxing the muscles in the pelvic area)  
they can extract their period and experience only the mildest of cramping immediately afterwards.  
There are a few possible hazards which can easily be avoided. Make certain that your cannula and speculum are sterile.
