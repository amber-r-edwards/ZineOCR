The staff woman was in charge of the work release program. At  
some time in serving a term you can apply for work release. That  
means you can work on campus when there are jobs opened or get a

you are qualified for work release, you have more privileges and  
being on the program brings you closer to parole. To get paroled  
you have to show you will be doing something -- school or work --  
when you get out. Since most prisoners couldn't afford school,  
they need a job to qualify for parole.

All in all I felt good about having visited the prison. I felt  
excited but I don't know why. I certainly couldn't say that pri-  
son is an exciting place to be. It seemed that way (exciting) to  
the staff because of their purposeful crusade of liberal reform  
but it couldn't be exciting to serve time there. Given the show  
the liberals put on for us, I didn't see much of the life the  
women there must live -- only the authority they must deal with.  
That would be hard for me. It would be hard to be guarded about  
your politics or your life in that atmosphere against the touchy-  
feely T-grouping authority there. And because of the leniency,  
because they don't hire unnecessary labor and don't seem to make  
the prisoners do too much, I could see going crazy from boredom.  
The conditions there don't seem cruel but it would be another  
story if you freaked out. They could send you to the state mental  
institution and the conditions there are probably not geared  
at giving you a positive experience like Rockwell City is trying.  
I imagine they could be pretty hostile if you didn't like the ex-  
perience they were breaking their necks to give you. After all  
they are trying to supply the best they know of, the closest that  
can be set up to a middle-class campus; and if you couldn't relate  
to that I could imagine them reacting to you -- an unthankful  
piece of poor bulldyke trash.  

The staff knows, I think, the futility of what they're doing.  
One staff member said he knew that the best a woman leaving Rock-  
well City could expect was to get married and be supported or she  
could expect to be back at Rockwell soon. But while she's there  
they're supplying her with a glimpse of how other people live and  
even if she buys it when she's there, when she gets out she'll  
soon learn it's a whole other ballgame. I doubt the women there  
are that oblivious to the contradictions and maybe that's why  
they're apathetic.  

I want to say that despite how liberal the place is and beside  
from what I felt about how badly that could affect you, prison  
would still be horrible and you'd want out. And also that their lib-  
eral and permissive authority is just another means of control. I  
think my excitement came from in a sense coming in touch with  
something that before had been an abstract -- an unknown that I  
feared. It wasn't as bad as I thought it would be and it wasn't as  
good either. But it did become real and not as frightening.  

I wake in middle-of-the-night terror

I sense the great weight of the prison  

pressing down on the little box of room I lie in

How often do women awake
