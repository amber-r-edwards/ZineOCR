We are Sibylline House--four women,  
one of us seven years old--in Edmonton,  
Alberta. We have been building a strong,  
stable women's house. Struggle! is com-  
ing out of ideas that we have evolved  

through building our house, reading women's  

papers, and being with women. Now we  
want to share some of those ideas, so  
here they are. We would appreciate it if  
our articles were only reprinted in their  
totality and were not quoted out of context.  

We have been distressed lately at some  
of the attitudes we have been picking up on  
from the women's papers we receive. The  
things we disagree with are (1) that middle-  
class is being equated with reformist and  
that middle-class women must either deny  
their class background or resign them-  
selves to being reformist, (2) glorification  
of lower and/or working class, and (3)  
that distinct class lines and a pure Marxist  
analysis fit a contemporary North American  
revolution.

Why should working-class women trust us anymore than we trust men?  

The women of Struggle say that "we (middle-class women) have  
to acknowledge the privileges of our class and not use them to  
oppress working-class women." That's a contradiction. Our priv-  
ileges, and the way we got them, are oppressive. It's not the  
kind of thing that you can simply acknowledge and then go on your  
way. What middle-class women cannot get into their (our) heads is  
that we'll pay for living so well for so long. Do you think we've  
all been treated so well by this society because we're inherently  
better than working-class people? We have participated in the  
capitalist society, we reap its benefits, and our continued parti-  
cipation perpetuates it. There's no other way to look at it. And  
we'll have to pay for our privileges.  

"'More oppressed than thou, therefore more revolutionary' is  
not the rule of political consciousness." The needs of the last  
shall come first. That seems to be the point. If a revolution  
does not include the most oppressed, if it's not made by the most  
oppressed, if it's just some concept that exists only in the heads  
of middle-class women, then it's not a revolution at all. It  
feels like a nice idea, like Mississippi Summer when all the

and confrontations when we should be struggling with each other."  
Isn't it just that our middle-class "sen-  
sibilities" are offended by raised voices and anger? We've got  
to get the pigshit out of our heads that equates confrontation  
Of course there's anger. Of course mid-  
dle-class women will be confronted. But we can't just say "Sis-
