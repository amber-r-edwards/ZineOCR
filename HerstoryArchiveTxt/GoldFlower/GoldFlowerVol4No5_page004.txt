asian women stereotypes  

The history of Asian American women is largely  
affected by American immigration laws. Through this  
history, the foundations of the oriental stereotype  
can be understood.  

Most of the stereotypes find their origins in the  
Anti-Chinese period of 1870-1900 in America. During  
this time racism took the form of anti-Chinese  
immigration laws, "Chinese Must Go" campaigns, 1892  
Geary Act denying equal protection of the courts.  
The phrase "not a Chinaman's chance" originated  
during this time and reflected the judicial treatment  
of Asian Americans. The immigration law history is  
too long to explain here. In 1882 the Chinese Exclusion  
Act was passed preventing the "undesirable" Chinese  
worker from coming over. It was the climax of yellow  
peril when thousands of Chinese families and workers  
were killed by vigilantes. The Immigration Act of  

1924 stated all non-whites except of African descent  
were forbidden entry.  

Few women were ever allowed entry which led to  
stealing women from China and selling them to  
brothels which paid off immigration officials.  

These women were the "slave girl" traffic frequently  
supervised by the tongs. Women of all classes were  
subject to kidnapping due to the desperate situation  
for Chinese males. Not until the 1930s was there a  
sizable proportion of Chinese females for families.  
So, immigration law was unjust as well as punitive  
by sex and class. Only workers and women were  
forbidden entry. But, few other Chinese came anyway,  
knowing of the unwelcome situation despite stories  
of the "Mountain of Gold."  

In sum, the Exclusion Laws set into motion forces  
that were to pollute Sino-American relations,  
perpetuate an unnatural migratory labor situation in  
California, depriving certain citizens of the right  
to a home (forbidden to own land) and family, and  
insulate the American people against a true under-  
standing of the Chinese (and other Asians) and their  

heritage. (See The Story of the Chinese in America,  
B. L. Sung.)
