March 8th is a day for  
all women everywhere. Together  
we will commemorate our struggle  
to the present and consider the

International Women's Day  
originated in this country and  
has gone almost unnoticed for  
years. On March 8th, 1857  
women garment-textile workers  
marched in New York to protest  
their 12-hour day; miniscule  
salaries and poor working  
conditions. As they moved from  
their poor living area into the  
more affluent part of the city,  
police broke up the march,  
arrested many of the women and  
in the ensuing chaos, some of  
the women were trampled.  

In March of 1860 the  
women garment-textile workers  
formed their first union. Forty-  
eight years later on March 8,  
1908 thousands of women from the  
needle trades industry marched  
again in New York. In sixty-one  
years they had not moved very far:  
the demands were the same, shorter  
hours, an end to child labor and  
the right to vote. In 1910 Clara  
Zetkin, a German Socialist leader  
asked the Second International to  
recognize March 8th as Inter-  
national Women's Day to  
honor the struggles of these  
women. Her resolution was  
enthusiastically approved.

essary from the Center.

On March 8th, 1917 women  
textile workers in Petrograd struck  
against the overlong bread lines,  
living and working conditions.  

Although International  
Women's Day has been celebrated in  
many countries all over the world,  
observance had lagged in the  
United States until last year.  

The emergence of the Women's  
Liberation Movement has focused  
our attention once again on the  
struggle of all women. We see  

the problem beginning with the  
socialization of the small child,  
progressing to the woman at work  
with inferior job status, inferior  

health care, and lack of access to  
information.
