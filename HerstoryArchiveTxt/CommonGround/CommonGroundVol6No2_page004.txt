How Can You Tell if it's a  
Good Day Care Program?  

Information supplied  
by the Child Care Re-  
source and Referral  
Center at the Downtown  
YWCA, 852-6124.  

A good day care program is  
one that meets the child's total  
developmental needs: physical,  
intellectual, social, and emo-  
tional. The program that nur-  
tures the child as she grows is  
not only one whose facilities  
and staff meet the highest stan-  
dards, but one which is concerned  
with the emotional climate which  
provides understanding and support  
to the growing child. This check-  
list is offered as a guide to assist  
in identifying programs which en-  
able the child to reach her full  
potential with a sense of self-worth.  

IS THE INDOOR AND OUTDOOR SPACE OF  
THE CENTER ADEQUATE?  

1. There is outdoor space with  
appropriate equipment for the de-  
velopment of the large muscles and  
room for children to run and climb,  
lift and carry, push and pull.  

2. Indoors there is provision  
for quiet and busy activities. Ma-  
terials and equipment are conveni-  
ently and attractively arranged.  
There are separate areas for library,  
housekeeping, block play, arts and  
crafts, science, music, and move-  
ment. Children's work is prominently  
displayed.  

3. There are cubbies for each child.  

4. Child-sized furniture and  
adequate equipment materials are  
available for all children according  
to age and level.  

5. Provision of spacing between  
cots during resting time.  

6. Room to isolate the ill child.  

IS THE PHYSICAL PLANT SAFE FROM ALL  
HAZARDS AND DOES IT MAINTAIN SANITARY  
CONDITIONS?  

1. Fire and safety precautions are  
in evidence.  

2. Premises have been inspected by  
appropriate fire and safety officers.  

3. Heating facilities are adequate  
and safe, and there is good ventilation.  

4. Precautions are made against  
accidents by covering radiators, fix-  
ing broken equipment, keeping walks  
free of ice, enclosing dangerous  
stairwells, and other possible dangers  
to children.  

IS THERE A GOOD HEALTH PROGRAM?  

1. Provision for health examina-  
tions for staff and children by pri-  
vate physician or community health  
agency.  

2. A plan for daily health inspec-  
tion by staff to detect illness or  
disease.  

3. Staff concerned about relationship  
between good health practices  
and child development.  

4. Program planned to provide a  
balance of quiet and busy activities  
and to offer benefits of sunshine and  
outdoor play activities.  

5. Nutrition needs supplied by well-  
balanced, attractive meals and snacks  
at appropriate intervals. Meal times  
are pleasant and relaxed, and casual  
conversation is encouraged. Staff eats  
with the children, and children partici-  
pate in meal preparation and clean-up.  

IS THERE EQUIPMENT AND PLAY MATERIAL  
AVAILABLE TO MEET THE CHILDREN'S DEV-  
ELOPMENTAL NEEDS, GROWTH, INTERESTS  
AND ENJOYMENT?  

1. There is material to encourage  
social and dramatic play, such as large  
and small blocks, wheeled toys, house-  
keeping accessories, mirrors, puppets,  
dolls, dress-up clothes, etc.  

2. There is climbing apparatus,  
balance boards, bicycles, etc., to  
foster physical and motor development  
and imaginative play.  

3. The child's initiative and self  
expression are stimulated by the avail-  
able media: water, sand, paints, clays,  
crayons, etc.  

4. Opportunities are provided for  
musical experience by singing,  
moving to music, listening to records,  
and playing a simple tone instrument.  

5. There is a wide variety of  
children's books, including pic-  
ture, poetry, and story books.  

6. There are games, puzzles,  
and toys to stimulate cognitive  
development.  

IS THERE SUFFICIENT STAFF?  

1. Staff-child ratios are con-  
sistent with standards for quality  
programs.  

2. The groups are supervised so  
that needs are met and each child  
is assured of physical care and  
individual attention.  

DOES THE PROGRAM PROVIDE UNDER-  
STANDING AND SUPPORT OF THE  
GROWING CHILD?  

1. There is a well-planned pro-  
gram which gives stability and reg-  
ularity to the day with sufficient  
variety and flexibility to provide  
for new experiences.  

2. The daily program is based  
on the child's readiness to absorb  
activities and concepts. Staff  
gives consideration to age level,  
attention span, motor ability, and  
intellectual capacity of the  
individual child.  

3. The child is encouraged to  
use the materials at the center:  
clay, paint, crayons, wood, etc.,  
creatively and with gradual skill  
to discover their unique qualities.  

4. The child is helped to gain  
facility in the use of language  
and is assisted in growth in  
concept development.  

5. The child is free to talk  
about her experiences and con-  
cerns with staff and other children.  

6. The child's understanding of  
the world around her is broadened  
through firsthand contacts, includ-  
ing trips and neighborhood excursions.  

7. The child is supported in her  
curiosity and inquisitiveness, and her  
questions are answered. She is helped  
to gain information from adults,  
children, books, and personal observa-  
tion.  

IS THERE PROVISION FOR THE CHILD'S  
SOCIAL DEVELOPMENT?  

1. The staff helps the child under-  
stand other children's rights and feel-  
ings and learn to take turns and to share.  

2. All children are made to feel  
that they belong to the group: the  
shy, the demanding, the aggressive, the  
exceptional, and the placid, and each  
is helped to feel at ease and develop  
assurance.  

3. Each child is encouraged to  
deal with and control her anxieties  
and hostilities through understand-  
ing, redirecting, and verbalizing them  
or acting them out.  

THE STAFF IS SENSITIVE AND ALERT TO  
THE DEVELOPMENTAL NEEDS OF THE CHILDREN  

1. The general atmosphere of the  
center is comfortable, pleasant, and  
friendly, and the child develops a  
sense of trust when the adults lis-  
ten to, respect, and respond to her.  

2. The sounds of the center re-  
flect children who are active and  
happy: talking, laughing, singing,  
hammering, etc. There is no stridency  
that would reflect pressure. Staff and  
children enjoy each other.  

3. The staff does not use shame,  
ridicule, or corporal punishment as  
a means of discipline.  

4. The staff tries to establish  
every child's status in the group by  
appreciation of her unique efforts.  
Staff understands the importance of  
praising and encouraging the child.  

5. The children are not always  
doing the same thing at the same  
time, and activities are geared to  
meet individual developmental levels  
and needs.  

IS STAFF TRAINED, COMPETENT AND  
CONCERNED?  

1. Staff members qualify to work  
with groups of children through  
training, experience, and willingness  
to work.  

2. Individual staff members show  
qualities of warmth, openness, flex-  
ibility, and genuinely accept and  
like children.  

3. The staff members display warmth  
and affection without artificial sent-  
imentality and favoritism.  

4. There is ongoing in-service  
and staff development.  

5. The training of staff has in-  
volved theoretical and practical  
work in child development, early  
childhood education, nutrition,  
parent education, and community.
