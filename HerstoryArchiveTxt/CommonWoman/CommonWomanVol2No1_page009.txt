TANGLED VINES  
for Lyn Lifshin  

Daughter, we were told  

was the common name of the orange tuberous  
weed like a deadly potato eye strangling  

the ivy bed. My mother  

could have been a botanist  

the way she studied plants  

gloxinia, bottle bush, bonsai, calla lily  

but for the parasite  

she had no fond regard.  

She had four daughters herself  

and understood, when my father could not hinder  

the vegetable tendrils carried by spores  
down from the gray and arid Sierra Madres  
by daily weeding or spraying,  
that it was time to get the experts.  
It bothered me to name a weed the thing  
we were, me and my sisters,  
as if it made a problem of us, too  
and to have my mother stare  
placidly through the living room window  
across the rising yard to the ivy  
and then up to the mountains from whence,  
she often said, cometh our help  
while outside the daughter  
was finally poisoned  
as cleanly stripped by the gardener  
from all that lush and miniaturized greenery  
as iris yellow grows away from stem.  

Marcia Goldberg
